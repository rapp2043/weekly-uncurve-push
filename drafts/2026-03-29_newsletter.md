SUBJECT LINE: The Pressure Paradox

Sarah Miller had seventeen minutes before her next appointment. She stood in the empty hallway of the alumni center, a cavernous building that smelled of old wood and lemon polish, staring at the name on her iPad screen: Robert J. Abernathy III, Class of ’78. Her notes said he was a “warm lead,” a former board member with a history of modest giving, now possibly ready for a major gift. Her director had been clear. This quarter’s initiative was “Aspirational Visits.” Every meeting with a prospective major donor, like this one, had to have an explicit, ambitious dollar figure attached to it before she even walked in the room. Sarah’s goal for the next forty-five minutes: secure a verbal commitment for a $250,000 endowed scholarship fund. The number was typed at the top of her notes, a silent, pulsating totem. She took a deep breath, smoothed her blazer, and pushed open the heavy oak door to the conference room where Mr. Abernathy waited, smiling politely. She had no way of knowing it, but the very goal designed to focus her efforts was about to make her far less likely to succeed.

We live in a world that worships goals. From corporate boardrooms to fitness apps, the mantra is the same: set a target, make it specific and ambitious, and let its gravitational pull draw you toward success. This philosophy is codified, celebrated, and seemingly incontrovertible. In philanthropy, this translates to a fundamental rule of major-gifts fundraising: always ask for a specific amount. Vagueness is the enemy of generosity. So when a team of researchers decided to examine the actual effectiveness of one of fundraising’s most intuitive practices—setting ambitious, specific visit-by-visit goals for gift officers—they expected to confirm what everyone already knew. They were instead handed a puzzle. A study of college fundraisers, published in the *Journal of Behavioral Decision Making*, found that officers who were required to set high, specific monetary goals for individual donor visits *raised less money* than those who were not. The very mechanism of motivation became a mechanism of sabotage. It turns out that what works for planning a quarter’s revenue can backfire catastrophically in the delicate, human dance of a one-on-one conversation. To understand why, we need to look past the spreadsheet and into the psychology of performance under a very particular kind of pressure.

Consider for a moment what you’re asking someone to do in a meeting like Sarah’s. You are not a cashier ringing up a sale. You are a storyteller, a listener, a matchmaker between a donor’s deepest interests and an institution’s needs. The transaction, if it happens, is emotional long before it is financial. The goal-setting theory pioneered by psychologists Edwin Locke and Gary Latham, which rightly dominates our management thinking, tells us that specific, challenging goals lead to higher performance on straightforward tasks.[^1] But is asking for a quarter of a million dollars a straightforward task? Or is it more like a pianist performing a concerto? One is a matter of procedure; the other is a complex, fluid performance where conscious thought is the enemy of fluency. The researchers hypothesized that the ambitious visit goal shifted the fundraiser’s cognitive focus. Instead of being absorbed in the donor’s story—listening for clues about their passion for marine biology or their late wife’s love of the theater—the officer’s mind becomes preoccupied with the number. *How do I steer this back to the ask? Is now the time? They mentioned their stock portfolio, was that an opening?* The goal becomes a cognitive load, crowding out the social and emotional intelligence required to navigate the conversation effectively. They stop listening and start calculating, a shift donors sense instinctively.

This phenomenon isn’t unique to fundraising. Psychologists who study performance have a name for it: *choking*. Not the informal kind, but a specific failure of skill under pressure. Sian Beilock, a cognitive scientist at the University of Chicago, has spent her career unpacking why experts falter. In her book *Choke*, she delineates a critical difference. For simple or newly learned tasks, pressure makes us try harder, often successfully. But for complex, well-practiced skills—like a fundraisers’ refined social art, or a golfer’s putting stroke—pressure causes us to overthink. We revert from smooth, automated proficiency to halting, conscious control. We “step outside ourselves” and start monitoring our own performance. The ambitious visit goal does exactly that to Sarah. It pulls her out of the flow of the conversation. She is no longer Sarah, the empathetic ambassador for her university; she is Sarah, the agent of a $250,000 target, watching herself perform. And as anyone who has ever botched a speech by thinking “I am giving a speech” knows, self-monitoring is where authenticity goes to die.

There is a second, more insidious layer at work here, one that involves not just cognition but motivation. The researchers pointed to what they called the “controlling” nature of specific, assigned goals. When a goal is handed down from a supervisor, it can undermine an individual’s sense of autonomy—their intrinsic feeling of being the originator of their own actions. This is the realm of Self-Determination Theory, championed by psychologists Edward Deci and Richard Ryan. Their decades of research show that intrinsic motivation—doing something because it is interesting, enjoyable, or aligns with personal values—is far more powerful and sustainable than extrinsic motivation driven by rewards, punishments, or, crucially, imposed targets. For many gift officers, the core joy of the job is the connection, the mission, the “lightbulb moment” when a donor sees how they can make a difference. A rigid visit goal can reframe the entire interaction in their own mind. It transforms a mission-driven conversation into a compliance activity. “I have to hit my number” replaces “I get to build a partnership.” That shift in internal narrative, from *want-to* to *have-to*, is a potent demotivator. It saps the very energy and authenticity that makes a fundraiser compelling.

So we have a cognitive trap and a motivational trap. But is the answer to have no goal at all? That’s where a third concept enters, one that feels almost like a compromise between the two extremes. It’s the idea of *optimal anxiety*, illustrated by the classic Yerkes-Dodson Law. Formed over a century ago, this psychological principle suggests that performance increases with physiological or mental arousal—but only up to a point. After that peak, further arousal causes performance to plummet. Picture an inverted U-shaped curve. On the far left, with no arousal, you have lethargy and inattention. At the peak of the curve, you have focused, energized, engaged performance. On the far right, you have debilitating, choking anxiety. The ambitious visit goal doesn’t move Sarah from the left side of the curve toward the peak. It rockets her straight past the peak to the far right. It swaps healthy focus for paralyzing pressure. The trick for managers, then, is not to eliminate goals, but to find the type of goal that creates the gentle slope of the left side of the curve, not the cliff of the right. This might mean setting broader, thematic goals for a visit—“discover his true philanthropic priority” or “rekindle his emotional connection to the biology department”—rather than a single, looming dollar figure.

Let’s return to Sarah in that conference room. She’s halfway through the meeting. Robert Abernathy is talking warmly about Professor Emeritus Henderson, who ignited his love for economics. The script in Sarah’s head says this is a perfect segue to the scholarship ask in Henderson’s name. But she’s listening now, not to her script, but to him. She hears a wistfulness, not just a fondness. He mentions he never got to thank Henderson properly before he passed. An idea forms, one entirely separate from the $250,000 goal burning in her folder. What if, she asks, he’d like to sponsor a guest lecturer series in Henderson’s honor? A way to bring that same inspirational teaching to today’s students? It would be a smaller, more immediate project. Abernathy’s eyes light up. “Now that,” he says, leaning forward, “is something I could get excited about.” They spend the rest of the meeting designing it. The commitment is $50,000. By the strict metric of her visit goal, Sarah has failed. But she has done something more important: she has planted a seed, built genuine rapport, and started a giving relationship that, history shows, is far more likely to grow into that major gift over time than a pressured ask that results in a polite “I’ll think about it” and a permanent cooling of interest.

The lesson here extends far beyond the hushed halls of university development offices. It speaks to any situation where complex human interaction is reduced to a single, high-stakes metric. The overzealous sales target that makes a representative pushy and deaf to a client’s real needs. The standardized test score goal that turns a teacher into a curriculum-drill sergeant, crushing student curiosity. In our quest to measure and motivate, we must distinguish between tasks that are truly mechanical and those that are, at their heart, artistic and human. Sometimes, the best way to hit a big target is to stop staring at it so intently, and to look, instead, at the person in front of you.

[^1]: The irony is that Locke and Latham’s own later work acknowledges the limits of their theory. They note that for complex tasks, learning goals—goals to *figure out how* to do something—are often superior to performance goals, which fixate on the specific outcome. The fundraising visit is the epitome of a complex task, yet we persistently treat it with a performance-goal mindset.
[^2]: This has a fascinating parallel in the history of philanthropy itself. Before professional fundraising, major gifts were often the result of long, meandering relationships without explicit “asks.” Andrew Carnegie’s library grants, for instance, famously came with strings attached, but the initial conversations were likely more about civic philosophy than a specific dollar figure. The modern, goal-oriented approach is a product of management science creeping into a deeply social realm.
