SUBJECT LINE: Why We're Getting Weaker

In the summer of 1993, a researcher named Annabel Thomas stood in a field in Hampshire, England, holding the thigh bone of a man who died in the 14th century. She was there with a team from the University of Cambridge, and they had brought a group of elite rowers from the university’s famed boat club. The plan was simple, if unusual: they would x-ray the rowers’ bones, and then they would x-ray the bones of the medieval farmer pulled from the clay. Thomas and her colleagues were interested in a specific metric: cortical area, the thickness of the dense outer shell of bone. It is a direct record of the load a skeleton has borne over a lifetime. The rowers, some of the strongest athletes in the world, possessed femurs of impressive density, honed by thousands of hours driving against the resistance of water. The medieval farmer, who spent his life plowing fields by hand, hauling timber, and grinding grain, made them look delicate. His cortical bone area was nearly 20% greater. The rowers were modern specimens of peak physical conditioning. The farmer was just an ordinary man, doing the ordinary work of his time. And he was, in this fundamental structural way, far more robust.

We carry a certain story about human evolution in our minds. It is a story of progress, of ascent. We got smarter. We got more sophisticated. We learned to manipulate our environment so thoroughly that we insulated ourselves from the brutal pressures of natural selection. The narrative, popularized for decades, is that once culture was invented, biological evolution essentially stopped. Our tools, our medicine, our shelters became the extensions that protected our fragile, unchanging biology. The engine of change shifted from our genes to our memes. It’s a comforting thought. It places us at the pinnacle, masters of our own destiny. But here’s what makes that fact about the medieval farmer’s bone so strange: it suggests that in certain measurable, physical ways, the direction of recent change isn’t toward greater resilience. It might be toward something else entirely.

This is not an isolated finding. Bioarchaeologists—scientists who study skeletal remains—have been quietly compiling a catalog of these regressions for years. The fossil record shows that since the peak of the last Ice Age, human brains have actually gotten smaller. The average cranial capacity has shrunk by about 10%, a loss roughly equivalent to the volume of a tennis ball. Our teeth and jaws have become more crowded and prone to disease as our diets softened. And our skeletons, like that of the Hampshire farmer, have become more gracile, lighter, less built for enduring sheer mechanical stress. This presents a puzzle. If evolution is about survival of the fittest, and we have become—in these concrete, structural terms—less fit, what exactly is going on? What force is driving this transition?

For a long time, the answer seemed to lie in a concept called “self-domestication.” The theory, drawing a parallel to how we selectively bred wolves for tameness and in the process got floppy ears and smaller teeth, posits that as human societies grew more cooperative, we selectively bred ourselves for pro-sociality and reduced aggression. The physical changes—the smaller face, the lighter build—were just side effects, a biological package deal with a calmer temperament. It’s an elegant idea. But it’s largely a story about the deep past, explaining changes over tens of thousands of years. It doesn’t fully explain the thigh bone of a 14th-century peasant outclassing that of a 20th-century Olympian. That shift happened in a blink, evolutionarily speaking. For that, we need to look at a more immediate, more pervasive pressure.

And this is where the story gets interesting. Because the driver of this rapid physical transition might not be a positive trait we were selecting *for*, like cooperation, but rather the gradual, wholesale removal of the thing that *used* to select for robustness: relentless, grinding physical challenge.

Consider a simple thought experiment. Imagine your daily caloric budget—the energy you can afford to expend—is terrifyingly tight. You are a subsistence farmer. Every joule of energy is precious. Now, your body faces a constant, low-grade mechanical stress: walking miles for water, tilling soil, chopping wood, grinding grain. Your skeleton adapts by building more bone, a process called modeling, in response to those loads. It’s an investment. Building and maintaining that robust skeleton is metabolically expensive. It requires extra calories and nutrients. But the cost is justified because the alternative—a bone that breaks under load—is catastrophic.

Now, introduce a series of innovations. A well is dug in the village center. A metal plow pulled by an oxen replaces the hand hoe. A mill powered by a stream grinds the grain. Each innovation is a masterpiece of problem-solving. Each makes life more comfortable, more secure, and vastly more efficient. But each also, silently, removes a non-negotiable reason for your body to invest in that expensive, robust skeleton. The mechanical signal that told your bones to thicken—the daily weight-bearing struggle—fades. In the absence of that signal, the metabolic calculus flips. In a world where calories can still be scarce, why spend them on structural overengineering? The body, in its brilliant, short-sighted efficiency, begins to divert resources elsewhere, or simply saves them. The skeleton becomes adequate for the new, reduced demands placed upon it. It becomes, in the word bioarchaeologists use, *gracile*.

This isn’t just theory. We can see this process playing out in real time in our own era. Take height, a classic marker of health and nutritional status. For over a century in the developed world, we grew taller and taller, a phenomenon known as the secular trend, fueled by better nutrition and reduced childhood disease. But in some of the tallest populations on Earth, like in the Netherlands, that trend has stalled or even started to reverse. Research by scientists like Gert Stulp has suggested a counterintuitive culprit: the very success of our modern, comfortable world. With obstetrical care mitigating the risks, there may be little evolutionary penalty for being extremely tall, but there’s also diminishing returns on the massive caloric investment required to get there. The pressure to be *maximally* tall, driven by past nutritional scarcity, relaxes. The body settles into a new, slightly lower equilibrium.

The most profound expression of this principle, however, may be unfolding in the very process that defines mammalian evolution: childbirth. For decades, the accepted story was the "obstetrical dilemma." It posited that humans pay a terrible price for our big brains and bipedal posture. The birth canal, narrowed for walking upright, conflicts with the large head of a big-brained baby. Evolution, the theory went, struck a compromise: human babies are born neurologically premature and helpless so they can pass through the canal. It was a classic tale of evolutionary trade-off.

But recent work is turning that idea on its head. In 2015, a team led by anthropologist Holly Dunsworth proposed a different framework: the *energetics of gestation*. They argued that the limiting factor isn’t pelvic size, but maternal metabolism. A pregnant human mother’s metabolic rate peaks at about twice her baseline. To grow a fetus for a significantly longer period would push her into an unsustainable, dangerous energy deficit. So, the baby is born when the mother’s body can no longer afford to fuel its growth internally. The “dilemma” isn’t a geometric puzzle of hips and heads; it’s a metabolic budget.

And this is where our modern comfort re-enters the picture, with potentially dramatic consequences. If the primary constraint on gestation length and newborn size is maternal energy budget, what happens when that budget expands? What happens with consistent, high-calorie nutrition, the elimination of heavy physical labor during pregnancy, and advanced medical intervention that can safely manage the risks of longer gestation and larger newborns?[^1]

The pressure shifts. The old metabolic ceiling lifts. The evolutionary brake—the mother’s inability to physically sustain a larger fetus—is eased. There is emerging, though still debated, evidence that this may be happening. Average birth weights have increased in many developed countries over the past decades. Head circumferences at birth may be increasing as well. If this trend continues, and if it has a genetic component that is then passed on, we could be witnessing the early stages of a biological response to a profound cultural shift. The driver isn’t a new predator or a changing climate. It is the systematic removal of energetic and mechanical constraints that have shaped our bodies for millennia. Comfort, it seems, might be its own powerful selective force.

We think of evolution as a battle against nature. But what happens when we win that battle? The story of our recent biological transition suggests we don’t just stop changing. We change in response to the victory itself. The pressure is no longer “Adapt or die.” It becomes subtler, a silent accounting of resource allocation in a world of our own making. Why build a skeleton that can withstand a lifetime of hauling water if a plastic pipe brings it to your kitchen? Why limit fetal growth to protect a metabolically strained mother if her diet is abundant and her doctor is monitoring her?

This brings us back to Annabel Thomas and her medieval farmer. That man’s formidable femur was not a badge of superior genetics or evolutionary advancement. It was a receipt. A receipt for a life of inescapable, daily labor. The elite rower’s bone, for all its trained strength, was a product of optional, time-limited exertion. The farmer’s body had no choice but to invest; the rower’s body could choose where to allocate its resources. The difference between them is the story of the last seven hundred years. We have not escaped evolution. We have merely changed its terms. The unexpected characteristic driving our transition is the absence of the old, brutal demands. We are evolving, in fits and starts, into a species shaped not by the struggle to survive nature, but by the metabolic and mechanical logic of the comfortable world we built to escape it.

[^1]: This is a profoundly complex area. While some studies note increases in birthweight and cephalic size, disentangling environmental factors like diabetes from potential evolutionary trends is extraordinarily difficult. Furthermore, the rise of cesarean sections introduces another variable, potentially allowing for the survival and reproduction of genes that predispose to difficult births—a process some researchers call the "obstetrical paradox." The interplay between culture and biology here is a tangled, fascinating knot.
