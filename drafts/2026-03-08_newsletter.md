SUBJECT LINE: The Unseen Hand in the Machine

Kathleen Siminyu was trying to teach a machine to listen. It was 2019, and in her work with the African Institute for Mathematical Sciences, she was grappling with a problem that, on its surface, sounded purely technical: building speech recognition for African languages like Swahili and Luganda. The challenge wasn't just about processing sound waves or phonemes. It was about something more fundamental. The dominant speech recognition systems—the ones that power our smart speakers and dictate our messages—were built on vast databases of spoken English. They understood certain cadences, certain phonetic structures, certain ways of forming a question or a command. To train an AI to understand Swahili, Siminyu didn't just need more Swahili audio. She needed to confront a foundational assumption embedded in the technology itself: that the Western way of organizing and explaining speech was the default, the neutral, the universal starting point. She was, in effect, not just building a tool, but negotiating with a cultural legacy she never asked for.

We like to think of artificial intelligence as a creature of pure logic. It ingests data, detects patterns, and produces outputs. Its explanations—the increasingly important “why” behind its decisions—should be clean, mathematical, and culturally sterile. A credit algorithm denies a loan because of metrics X, Y, and Z. A diagnostic tool flags a tumor based on pixel density and morphology. The reasoning feels like it comes from a placeless, history-less realm of number and fact. This is the comforting promise of the machine: objectivity, untainted by human prejudice.

But there’s a problem with that theory.

In early 2024, a team of researchers from the University of the West of England and the University of Bristol published a curious study. They weren’t looking at the decisions AI made, but at the way it justified them. They presented people from Western cultures (like the UK and US) and Eastern cultures (like China and Japan) with AI-generated explanations for simple decisions, like recommending a book or a restaurant. The explanations came in different flavors. Some were direct, individualistic, and focused on the attributes of the choice itself—“This book has a complex protagonist.” Others were holistic, interdependent, and considered the social context—“People in your network enjoyed this book.” The findings were stark. Participants strongly preferred explanations that matched their own cultural worldview. Westerners wanted the direct, attribute-focused reasoning. Easterners preferred the holistic, socially-aware rationale. Here’s the twist: the AI systems themselves, trained largely on data and text from the internet—which is disproportionately generated by Western voices—were inherently better at generating the first kind of explanation. They were fluent in the language of Western individualism and struggled to articulate the logic of interdependence. The machine’s “objective” reasoning had an accent. A distinctly Western one.

So how did a global technology, born in the 21st century, learn to explain the world like a philosophy major from California? To understand that, we need to rewind the tape. Not to the birth of computing, but to the birth of the idea that how we explain things can be neutral in the first place.

Consider the case of John Bulwer, a 17th-century English physician. In 1644, decades before Isaac Newton published his *Principia*, Bulwer wrote a strange and ambitious book called *Chirologia: or the Natural Language of the Hand*. In it, he attempted to catalogue every possible hand gesture, arguing that they constituted a universal language of passion, one that transcended spoken tongues. Bulwer was a product of his time, a time of European exploration and colonization. As he “collected” gestures, he inevitably interpreted them through the rigid social and religious lens of Puritan England. A gesture common in the Mediterranean might be classified by Bulwer as “immodest”; one used by the Irish as “barbarous.” He believed he was discovering a neutral, biological lexicon of the body. In reality, he was constructing a taxonomy deeply stained with his own culture’s values and prejudices, and then presenting it as natural law. He couldn’t see his own hand in the work.

This instinct—to mistake one’s own cultural framework for a universal framework—isn’t confined to Renaissance gesture manuals. It permeates the very building blocks of modern science. In 1872, Charles Darwin published *The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals*. It was a landmark text, arguing for the evolutionary continuity of emotional life. To prove that expressions like fear or disgust were innate and universal, Darwin circulated a questionnaire to missionaries and colonial administrators scattered across the British Empire. He asked them to describe how the “natives” reacted in certain situations. The answers he got back were, of course, filtered through the interpreters’ own cultural perceptions and biases. A grimace of concentration might be recorded as a frown of anger; a ritualized display of grief might be misread as hysterical fear. Darwin, brilliant as he was, was building a global theory of human expression on data collected through a profoundly narrow cultural keyhole. He believed he was mapping human nature. He was, in part, mapping the assumptions of Victorian England onto the rest of the world.

Now, take a leap forward to the mid-20th century and the dawn of artificial intelligence. The field’s pioneers, people like Alan Turing, John McCarthy, and Marvin Minsky, were working with a potent, culturally specific metaphor: the mind as a computer. Reasoning was formal logic. Knowledge was a database of facts. Explanation was a logical proof. This was a powerful idea, but it was also a particular one, rooted in a Western philosophical tradition that prizes abstraction, deduction, and the separation of the individual knower from the social world. When these pioneers dreamed of machine intelligence, they were dreaming of a machine that reasoned like the most idealized version of a Western academic. The cultural soil in which the seed of AI was planted was not neutral. It was already rich with specific assumptions about what intelligence *is* and, crucially, what a good explanation *sounds like*.

Fast forward to today, to the large language models like ChatGPT. They are trained on terabytes of text from the internet—from Wikipedia articles to Reddit threads to digitized books. This corpus is often called “the sum of human knowledge.” But is it? Or is it the sum of *digitized*, *predominantly English-language*, *often Western-produced* human knowledge? The internet’s architecture, its dominant platforms, and its most voluminous contributors skew heavily toward North America and Europe. When an AI learns language from this corpus, it doesn’t just learn grammar and facts. It learns norms. It learns that a persuasive argument often starts with a direct thesis statement. It learns that attributing success to individual grit is a common narrative trope. It learns that explaining a decision by referencing personal preference is more common than referencing familial duty. It absorbs, at a scale Bulwer or Darwin could never have imagined, the explanatory habits of the cultures that produce the most data.

This brings us back to the puzzle of the 2024 study. The AI’s pro-Western explanatory bias isn’t a bug in a single algorithm. It’s a feature of its lineage. It is the logical endpoint of a centuries-old tradition of mistaking a particular cultural viewpoint for a universal one, now supercharged by data and scale. The “unseen hand” in the machine isn’t just code; it’s history.

Let’s play a game. Imagine you are an AI trained entirely on the corporate filings, earnings call transcripts, and business journalism of the last 50 years. You are asked to explain why a company failed. What stories are you most likely to tell? You’d probably talk about strategic missteps, competitive pressures, maybe poor leadership. These are the narratives that dominate that dataset. Would you ever explain a bankruptcy by referencing a shift in community values, or the loss of a shared sense of purpose among employees, or the founder’s obligation to a family legacy over shareholder return? Those explanations exist in human cultures, but they are whispers in the thunderous data of modern Western business discourse. The AI, statistically, will reach for the loudest, most repeated explanatory tools in its kit. And those tools were forged in a particular workshop.

This is the subtle tyranny of the default. For Kathleen Siminyu and her colleagues working on African speech AI, the challenge isn't merely technical. It's archaeological. They must sift through the cultural layers embedded in the very tools and datasets they use, trying to separate computational logic from inherited worldview. They are not just programmers; they are translators, negotiating between the ingrained explanatory preferences of the technology and the different, equally valid, ways of knowing and reasoning that exist in the world.

The promise of AI was to free us from human bias. But it turns out that to do that, we must first confess a deeper, more ancient bias: the bias of believing our own way of seeing the world is the world itself. The machine isn't escaping history. It's holding up a mirror to it, showing us the enduring shape of our own assumptions, now cast in silicon and scale. Siminyu’s work, then, is more than innovation. It is a form of correction. A quiet, determined effort to ensure that when the machine finally learns to listen, it can hear more than just its own, long-rehearsed, culturally-specific story.

[^1]: This phenomenon even has a name in digital humanities: *digital colonialism*. It refers to the way the infrastructures, datasets, and norms of the digital age can perpetuate the cultural and economic dominance of the nations that created them, effectively creating new forms of dependency.
[^2]: A poignant example is the field of "affective computing," which teaches machines to recognize human emotion. Many systems are trained on databases of facial expressions codified from Western subjects, leading to comical and sometimes consequential errors when interpreting expressions from other cultures where, for instance, a smile might not indicate happiness, or a widened eye might not indicate fear.
