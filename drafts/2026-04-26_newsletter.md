SUBJECT LINE: Why Wheelchair Basketball Proves Everything We Think About Sex Differences Is Wrong

The researcher had spent years immersed in the world of sports science, studying performance differences between male and female athletes. They knew the numbers cold: in running, men outpace women by about 11 percent. In swimming, the gap is about 10 percent. In weightlifting, it widens to nearly 30 percent. These numbers feel like laws of nature, as immutable as gravity. But then the researcher watched a wheelchair basketball game, and something strange happened. They could not tell, just by watching, who was male and who was female. The speed of the passes, the height of the jumps off the chair, the sprint down the court—none of it gave it away. And that observation, once they began to investigate it, would upend everything they thought they knew about sex, sport, and the human body.

So what was going on? The researcher decided to study it systematically. They gathered data from wheelchair basketball competitions at the Paralympic level, timing sprint speeds, measuring throwing accuracy, tracking game performance. What they found was startling. In able-bodied basketball, there is a well-documented performance gap: men’s fastbreak speed is about 15 percent faster, vertical jump is about 20 percent higher, and three-point shooting percentages are higher too. But in wheelchair basketball, the gap nearly vanished. In some measures, the best female athletes matched or even outperformed their male counterparts. The difference in speed over a 20-meter sprint was less than 3 percent. The accuracy of passes and shots was statistically indistinguishable. It turns out that when you strip away the biological advantages that come from running and jumping on two legs—when you force everyone to sit in a chair and push with their arms—the inherent physiological differences between men and women become dramatically less relevant.

That is a radical idea. It suggests that the performance differences we see in able-bodied sports are not purely a reflection of biology. They are, at least in part, a reflection of the *equipment* we use, and the way that equipment amplifies certain biological traits. A running shoe does not discriminate, but the act of running does: longer legs, larger lungs, and greater muscle mass give a disproportionate advantage. A wheelchair, on the other hand, demands upper-body strength, coordination, and technique—traits that are far more evenly distributed between the sexes. As the researcher put it in their article, “When you change the interface between the athlete and the environment, you change the rules of the game.” And once you change the rules, the old assumptions no longer hold.

But here’s the problem. We have been raised on a steady diet of athletic statistics that seem to confirm biological destiny. We watch the Olympics and see that the fastest men in the world run the 100 meters in 9.58 seconds, while the fastest women run it in 10.49. We nod our heads and say, “Of course, men are faster.” We assume that this gap is fixed, that it will always be there, that it’s written into our DNA. And the truth is, for the sport of running, that assumption is probably correct. But what if the sport itself is the problem? What if we designed sports that neutralized the very advantages that men have? The wheelchair basketball example gives us a glimpse of what that world might look like.

Now, let’s think about this more broadly. Consider another sport: shooting. In Olympic rifle events, men and women compete separately, and the scores are often very close—within a few tenths of a point. But if you look closely, you’ll notice that in mixed-gender events, women sometimes win outright. The reason is simple: steady hands and precise aim are not strongly correlated with testosterone. Yet we still separate the sexes in shooting, even though the performance gap is almost nonexistent. Why? Because we are culturally conditioned to see sexes as separate categories, even when the evidence doesn’t require it. The wheelchair basketball research exposes that same cultural conditioning. It forces us to ask: In how many other sports are the performance gaps really about biology, and in how many are they about the *way* we play them?

Let me give you a thought experiment. Imagine you were asked to design a sport where men and women could compete on perfectly equal footing. What would that sport look like? It could be a game on wheels, like wheelchair basketball. It could be a game of fine motor skill, like archery or curling. It could be a game where endurance outweighs raw power, like ultramarathoning—where, interestingly, women often match or beat men over extreme distances. The point is, the options are real, and they exist. Yet we don’t think of them as the “normal” sports. We think of basketball, football, soccer—sports that happen to favor male anatomy. And because those sports dominate our cultural imagination, we treat the performance gaps they produce as universal truths.

This brings us to a deeper insight, one that has been explored by scholars like Cordelia Fine and others. Our brains are wired to find patterns, and once we find a pattern (men are faster than women) we tend to treat it as a fixed law of nature. We ignore the exceptions, we dismiss the role of training and opportunity, and we forget that many of these “laws” are relatively recent historical inventions. For example, women were not allowed to run the marathon in the Olympics until 1984. When they finally did, their times have dropped by over 30 percent since the 1970s, while men’s times have plateaued. That is not a biological phenomenon—it’s a social one. The wheelchair basketball study adds another layer: not just social changes, but *mechanical* changes can reorder the performance hierarchy completely.

So where does that leave us? The researcher who started with a simple observation—that wheelchair basketball players seemed equally matched regardless of sex—ended up challenging one of the most deeply held assumptions in sports science. Their work suggests that the performance gap between male and female athletes is not a fixed biological reality but a contingent one, dependent on the tools we use. And if that is true, then the entire framework of sex segregation in sports—which is intended to ensure fairness—may actually be enforcing a particular set of biological advantages that are only relevant in certain activities. It is a classic case of what the sociologist Murray Davis called a “theory that denies the obvious.” The obvious is that men and women are different and those differences show up in sports. The denied truth is that those differences are dramatically amplified or mitigated by the environment we create.

And the researcher is not alone. In recent years, a growing number of studies have examined the role of equipment and task design in sex differences. One study, published in the *Journal of Sports Sciences*, looked at rowing and found that when both sexes used identical ergometers, the performance gap shrank by half compared to on-water racing—where technique and boat design play a bigger role. Another study on weightlifting found that when male and female athletes lifted relative to their own body weight, the gap in some lifts dropped to almost zero. The wheelchair basketball example is just the most vivid illustration of a broader principle: the body is not a static machine. It adapts to the tools we give it. And when we change the tools, we change what the body can do.

Now, let’s return to the researcher’s original observation. They are sitting in a gymnasium, watching wheelchair basketball players fly up and down the court. They cannot tell the difference between the men and the women. If you had asked them a week earlier whether that was possible, they would have said no. They would have assumed that any sport that requires strength, speed, and coordination will inevitably favor men. But the evidence was right there in front of them: men and women on wheels, moving at the same speed, throwing with the same accuracy, competing with the same intensity. And the researcher had to admit that the obvious was not so obvious after all. The challenge now is to figure out what that means for the rest of the world—for how we train young athletes, how we design sports programs, and how we think about fairness. It is a puzzle that demands we set aside our comfortable assumptions and look at the data with fresh eyes.[^1] Maybe the most important lesson of all is this: when we think we are measuring biology, we are often measuring the intersection of biology and design. And design, unlike biology, can be changed.

[^1]: It is worth noting that the wheelchair basketball study does not suggest that all sports should be coed. There are still sports where raw power and speed matter enormously, and where the biological gap remains large even with equipment changes. The point is that the gap is not universal, and our decision to segregate sports should be based on empirical evidence, not on a blanket assumption.

[^2]: The history of wheelchair basketball itself is fascinating. It was invented by World War II veterans at the VA hospital in Van Nuys, California, in 1946. From the start, it was a mixed-gender activity, though today Paralympic wheelchair basketball is still divided by sex. The researcher’s findings raise the question: should it be?

[^3]: This same line of reasoning applies to transgender athletes. If the source of advantage is not fixed but malleable, then blanket bans based on sex chromosomes may be less justified than we assume. But that’s a separate—and very contentious—discussion.
